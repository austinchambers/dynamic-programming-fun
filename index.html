But instead of trying to consider all the activities at once, we can divide the larger problem into much smaller subproblems. Let's start with the smallest subproblem which is just one activity in 1 hour. Try it here:

You just solved all the possibilities for one activity, breaking everything down into a series of subproblems. This is the first step in Dynamic Programming.

After scheduling the date, there is one hour left. To get the maximum value for the time remaining, you reuse your previous solution from the corresponding time left in the row above.

To visualize this better, play around with the following table that we continuned for you. Notice how the optimal values for the remaining time are taken from the previous row.

In the table above, hover over the bottom-right square on the value of 9. This value is created by first considering the hike (value 5), which takes 4 hours. You have 7 hours total, so the max value for the remaining time of 3 hours is calculated by reusing your solution from the row above (value 4). These two values (5 and 4) are added to get your current max value of 9.

Finally, let's consider the beach, our last activity. Suppose we have seven hours free. Should we go to the beach?

When we have 7 hours and all of our activities, going to the beach and the gym does not maximize our fun. Taking the beach out of our schedule allows us to scheudule the date and the hike, for a max value of 9 instead of 8. In this case, taking our previously solved solution for the entire 7 hours from the row above is our optimal solution.

Sadly, to maximize our fun, we don't go to the beach!

This is actually a base case, something that can be solved without relying on a previously solved subproblem. We can now complete our table by adding a column of zeros on the left. Check it out below:

As you hover over the table, notice that all the base cases are in the top row and left column. You do not need any other solutions to solve these. However, all the other squares require other values other than themselves for their solutions, but only from their own row or the previous row of saved values.

We could continue adding time and activities, and using a Dynamic Programming approach, we would reach our optimal solution every time, even if we had hundreds of activities and many more hours.

These core concepts in Dynamic Programming are used in countless applications in computer science, mathematics, management science and bioinformatics. They simply involve a method for solving a complex problem by breaking it down into a collection of simpler subproblems, solving each once, then saving and reusing each solution to solve a larger problem.

But more importantly, we want you to see how Dynamic Programming can be useful and easy to do, especially when it comes to maximizing your fun!
